Profile of Dr. A. Majeed Khan

Dr. A. Majeed Khan, born at Faridpur in 1929 educated in Kolkata (India), London and Minnesota, USA has the distinction of being the first Ph.D. in Sociology & Anthropology (1958), in Bangladesh.

While finishing his doctorate in rural sociology and applied anthropology, at the University of Minnesota, USA in 1958, Khan was selected as one of nine young scholars - all in social sciences with some statistical background – together with legendary Akhter Hameed Khan, to plan and execute a rural development strategy principally drawing upon American rural development experience, by establishing the Academy for Rural Development, in Comilla, Bangladesh. Khan worked at this Academy till mid-1964. In this period, through field research and demonstration/action projects he developed different strategies of integrated family planning program together with comprehensive rural development incorporating women’s participation in home economics, family health welfare. Most of those programs and efforts were later incorporated into two successive 5-Years’ national socio-economic development Plans of 1965 and 1970s (pre-Independence).  
In the summer of 1964, Khan was invited to deliver the keynote address on Agricultural development in Asia at the International Conference of Rural Sociology held in Dijon, France. In the remaining year he served as a part-time teacher at the Institute of Education of the University of Stockholm and simultaneously, as a consultant to Swedish International Development Agency (then known as NIB; later renamed as SIDA).

Khan’s first experiment with higher education dates back to 1964 when he established the School of Social Work at Rajshahi University, with an innovative curriculum which provided for hands-on-experience to classroom teaching, In the same years he was appointed an Adviser to Pakistan National Planning Commission on health, education and social development; when simultaneously he held executive positions in several national voluntary social and professional organizations (eg: Vice President, Pakistan Public Health Association, Pakistan National Council of Social Welfare, National Commission for UNESCO).

In 1968, Khan was appointed by the United Nations, as (again) one of nine ‘inter-regional’ officers -other eight recruited as one from Eastern Europe and two from Latin America and one each from United Kingdom, USA, Canada, Egypt, India; and Khan from Pakistan - to provide policy and program support to member-governments to focus on human resources development to optimize socio-economic growth of respective countries.

Following a six weeks brain-storming at UN Headquarters in New York and two weeks briefing at UN agencies’ in Paris (UNESCO), Geneva (ILO & WHO) and Rome (FAO), Khan was posted to Indonesia (in the reconstruction phase after the fall of legendary President Soekarno) with concurrent responsibility for Malaysia, Singapore and Brunei. At the end of two years in that assignment, in 1971 Khan’s job responsibility was extended to cover all United Nations member countries in south-east Asia, particularly adding Thailand Korea and the Philippines to the previous charge with the relocated posting at ECAFE. in Bangkok,

After a period of 18 months in that post, in mid-1972 Khan was transferred to New York as a Senior Program Officer to help structure the newly formed United Nations Population Fund (UNFPA) under the leadership of Rafael Salas (a Pilipino and a former Executive Secretary (i.e. the Prime Minister) of the Country). After one year in that post, Khan was transferred to Sri Lanka as the first Regional Coordinator of UNFPA to help develop and execute an inter-agencies’ six million dollars 5-years’ Population Development Program in Sri Lanka. After 4 years on the post, Khan was transferred to the region of West Asia, again as the Regional Coordinator of UNFPA as the first time input from the United Nations system, to develop policy and programs on integrated population and development. For working in this vast region, Khan chose his own duty station Amman, the capital of Jordan in deference to Beirut (UN agencies regional headquarters) or Damascus, the largest metropolitan city in the region. From Amman, he served the Gulf States, Jordan, Syria, Iraq, Oman, and two Yemens – north and south on planning for health policy, education and social development.  

While still serving with the United Nations Development Program (UNDP) and agencies in the personal rank of a senior Director (D.2) he was recalled home in March 1982 to join the Government as the Minister of Education, and concurrently the Minister of Culture & Sports, Science and Technology, and the Minister of Religious Affair; and on a second stint, Minister of Planning (1984-85). From 1985 to 1987 he was Bangladesh Ambassador to France Spain and Morocco and Ambassador cum Permanent Representative of Bangladesh to the UNESCO.
In 1988 he took up teaching again as Visiting Professor in the Centre of Strategic Studies at the University of Jordan, Amman. Later, in 1989, he was Guest Professor at the International Institute of Education, the University of Stockholm, Sweden. While still with the UN, Khan served a summer (1977) as a visiting professor at the University of Leiden, Holland.   
In the period with the United Nations, the government; and privately, Khan has chaired and participated in many international conferences and seminars. Among others, he served as an elected member of Executive Committee of the UNESCO (1983-87), attended the Ministerial meeting of the Non-Aligned Committee on Palestine in 1983, (held in Delhi); Vice Chairman, Fourth Extraordinary Session of the General Conference of the UNESCO, held in Paris in November-December 1984, Chairman of the First Committee of the 24th General Conference of UNESCO (1987); Chairman of the Regional Program Review Committee of the South Asia Region, International Planned Parenthood federation (IPPF),  1989-1995; a member of the Advisory Board of Asia Fellows Program (jointly sponsored by the Institute of International Education (IIE/USA) and Ford Foundation (1999-2002).

Post-retirement and drawing upon his experience of higher education needs and quality cum accreditation experience through the UNESCO; and experience as the Minister of Education at home, Khan, in consultation with a number of eminent educationists in the country, in the States and the United Kingdom; and in responding to an appeal from a pioneering generation of young industrialists – mostly parents of school-finishing teen-aged children - and a scion of a noble family friend with an offer of 21 acres of land for a proposed university,  drafted a scheme of establishing a private university on the basis of the bludgeoning economic need and social aspirations of the emerging youth of the country. It was called the Independent University Bangladesh (IUB) – founded in 1991 and registered in 1993). It was modeled after American higher education to reflect two critical qualities: (a) to root in its community and (b) engage in search for knowledge. National Professor M. Innas Ali, a renowned Physicist was appointed as the first Rector (honorary) of IUB. While at IUB, Khan spent a semester (1997) as a Senior Fulbright Fellow at Hamline University, St. Paul, Minnesota, USA

Spending 7 years as the President of IUB after producing 3 batches of 4-years graduates in subjects like business administration, communication and environmental management, Khan retired from IUB and went to University of Wisconsin, Madison as a Resident Fellow in the Department of Rural Sociology in the College of Agriculture. While at Wisconsin he organized a special session on the issues of emerging ‘globalization’ at the Annual Conference of American Rural Sociological Society (1990) held in Washington D.C. In the same year, Khan joined an international team of Congressional Lobby of eminent people from Columbia, Zambia, Nepal and Bangladesh to plead with the Congress and the President (Clinton) to pass the Population Bill before the US Congress. (The Bill was passed by President Clinton, later rescinded for that year by his successor, President Bush).   
While at Madison he also closely studied the scope and functions of information technology in the emerging global society. So, upon return to Dhaka, in 2002, he initiated and later in 2003 established a second private university; the University of Information Technology and Sciences: UITS. That was the first private University of IT and Sciences in the country offering Bachelor degree in subjects like Electrical and Electronic Engineering (EEE) or Information Technology even before Bangladesh University of Engineering and Technology (BUET). However, soon this venture turned into a “business” operation and Khan resigned from the post of Vice Chancellor and left UITS before taking the first batch of students through graduation!

Meantime, he was invited by the Ministry of Education to participate in a study to help-produce a Strategic Plan for Higher Education in Bangladesh: 2006-2026 –a study participated by some 40 scholars and specialists and a selected number of corporate representatives. Khan, together with a team of experts outlined the vision, size and shape of that 20 Years’ Plan! This initiative has since resulted in the creation of an Academic Innovation Fund (AIF) of a billion dollar grant from the World Bank (first time on its agenda) to support projects to ensure quality higher education in the country. The AIF operates under the guidance of the University Grants Commission (UGC).
Related to educational entrepreneurship, Khan has received numerous academic and civil awards including an honorary doctorate: 'Doctor of Humane Letters' from Hamline University, St. Paul, Minnesota, USA also, Phi Beta Kappa – the highest academic distinction for promoting liberal education; and a decoration: Officier dan ordre des Palmes Academiques, by the Government of the Republic of France. Most recently he has been elected a member of the Advisory Council of AUAP (Association of Universities of Asia and Pacific - ASEAN).
Khan has authored more than 100 professional articles, monographs and book chapters; and some years back edited a compendium of biographical work: Twenty Great Bengalis (UPL, Dhaka) of a millennium from Atish Dipankar ( a Budhist Scholar) to Nobel Laureate Professor Muhammad Yunus (for Peace, 2006). Currently, he is engaged in co-editing an enlarged second edition of National Culture and Heritage - a collection of essays by distinguished authors and scholars of the country.  
All his life Khan has been actively involved in different socio-cultural activities. He has been a President of Lions International also a Rotary Club (Buriganga, Dhaka), member of the Royal Golf Club, Colombo, the Jordan Archeological Society, and many more learned bodies and professional organizations, He is a life member of Asiatic Society of Bangladesh, life member Bangladesh Itihas Parisad, and Family Planning Association of Bangladesh. For nearly a decade, Khan had been a Director of Chittagong Stock Exchange and President of Bangladesh Centre for Communication Program – a development communication research organization – a successor to Johns Hopkins University (Bultimore, Maryland, USA) Communication Research Division field unit in Bangladesh.                    

 

Since 2009 Khan has since been working with a group of like-minded scholars, scientists and social leaders to initiate a movement for a fast-track and holistic economic development of agro-based economy through research in biotechnology and synthetic biology. This initiative (since registered as non-political, not-for-profit, membership-based voluntary organization) is now named as Committee of Action for Research, Extension and Services – CARES, of which Dr. Khan is the current Executive Chairman

 

Dr. A. Majeed Khan is the President Emeritusof Independent University, Bangladesh (IUB), a member of the Board of Trustee of Chittagong Independent University (CIU); and a founding member of the Education, Science, Technology, and Cultural Development Trust (ESTCDT).
